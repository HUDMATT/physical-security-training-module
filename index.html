Don’t Hold the Door

How attackers use urgency, authority, and politeness to bypass access controls

Someone behind you says, "Hey, can you hold the door? I forgot my badge." If you open the door, they may have just gotten through your entire access control system.

Learning Objectives

Define Concepts

Understand tailgating and physical social engineering.

Recognize Techniques

Identify common methods attackers use.

Apply Best Practices

Make the safest and best decisions to keep the system secure.

What Is Tailgating?

Tailgating is when someone follows an authorized person through a locked entryway without using their badge or PIN. Piggybacking is similar but often involves the authorized person knowingly letting someone in.

Key Point: Access control only works if every person badges in individually.

Why People Fall For It

Being human inherently leaves us vulnerable. Attackers know this, and use those vulnerabilities to their advantage.
Human behavior vulnerabilities that attackers exploit:

Politeness

People do not want to seem rude by not letting someone through.

Assumption

"They look like they belong here."

Authority

"I'm from IT.", "I'm from management.", "I'm from facilities."

Urgency

"I'm late for a meeting."

Familiarity

"You've seen me around before."

Uniform Bias

Reflective vest, clipboard, tool bag, delivery box, or badge lanyard can make someone seem like they are supposed to be there.

Common Physical Social Engineering Techniques

What it looks like: Someone follows you through a door.
Why it works: Politeness and assumption.
What to do: Politely ask them to badge in.

What it looks like: Person in uniform with tools.
Why it works: Uniform bias and authority.
What to do: Verify their access through security or facilities.

What it looks like: Person claiming IT issue.
Why it works: Authority and urgency.
What to do: Do not grant access, report it to IT.

What it looks like: Person with package needing access.
Why it works: Urgency and assumption.
What to do: Direct them to reception.

What it looks like: Asking to borrow a badge.
Why it works: Politeness and familiarity.
What to do: Never lend your badge, no matter how much you know the person.

What it looks like: Watching someone enter PIN or badge.
Why it works: Opportunity.
What to do: Cover your inputs and report to security.

What it looks like: Searching trash for info.
Why it works: Poor disposal practices.
What to do: Shred any sensitive documents.

What it looks like: Lying to receptionist.
Why it works: Social engineering.
What to do: Verify everything they tell you and are asking for.

Why Badging Matters

Important: Every person must badge in every time. Even coworkers should badge in behind you.

A person who "looks familiar" may not be authorized today. They could be:

  • A recently fired employee
  • A contractor whose access expired
  • An employee in the wrong area
  • A visitor without escort
  • An insider threat
  • Someone using a stolen badge

Badging creates an audit trail. If an incident happens, logs help investigators determine who entered and when. Holding the door breaks all acountability.

"But I Know Them"

Security is not about being rude. It is about being consistent.

Tell them: "Sorry, everyone has to badge in, it's for our safety and security."

Offer to help them in another way: "I'll walk you to the front desk to get a new badge."

Emphasize: In order to stay secure, everyone has to follow the rules, it's not personal.
