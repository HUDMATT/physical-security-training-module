Don’t Hold the Door

How attackers use urgency, authority, and politeness to bypass access controls

Someone behind you says, "Hey, can you hold the door? I forgot my badge." If you open the door, they may have just gotten through your entire access control system.

Learning Objectives

Define Concepts

Understand tailgating and physical social engineering.

Recognize Techniques

Identify common methods attackers use.

Apply Best Practices

Make the safest and best decisions to keep the system secure.

What Is Tailgating?

Tailgating is when someone follows an authorized person through a locked entryway without using their badge or PIN. Piggybacking is similar but often involves the authorized person knowingly letting someone in.

Key Point: Access control only works if every person badges in individually.

Why People Fall For It

Being human inherently leaves us vulnerable. Attackers know this, and use those vulnerabilities to their advantage.
Human behavior vulnerabilities that attackers exploit:

Politeness

People do not want to seem rude by not letting someone through.

Assumption

"They look like they belong here."

Authority

"I'm from IT.", "I'm from management.", "I'm from facilities."

Urgency

"I'm late for a meeting."

Familiarity

"You've seen me around before."

Uniform Bias

Reflective vest, clipboard, tool bag, delivery box, or badge lanyard can make someone seem like they are supposed to be there.
